✦   A Love Story   ✦

Chloe
& Patrick

On being hopelessly, irreversibly, magnificently undone

❧
One

The Bookshop on Crane Street

Patrick first saw her on a Tuesday — which was, until that moment, his least favorite day of the week. She was standing in the narrow aisle of Crane Street Books with a novel pressed against her chest and her eyes squeezed shut, as though she was trying to absorb the whole story through her sternum. He stopped mid-stride and watched, a copy of some architecture tome dangling forgotten from his hand.

She opened her eyes. They were brown with a ring of amber at the center — like the moment right before a match catches fire. She didn't notice him watching. She simply slid the book back onto the shelf, tilted her head at the spine as though having a private disagreement with it, and turned down the next aisle.

He followed. Not in a strange way. In the way water follows gravity — helplessly, and without knowing there was ever another option.

"That one's devastating," he said, nodding at the shelf she'd just abandoned.

She turned, surprised, then looked back at the book. "I know," she said. "That's why I put it down. I'm not in a devastating mood."

"What kind of mood are you in?"

She thought about it seriously, which he immediately loved. "A Wednesday mood," she said finally. "Something hopeful. Not too loud."

He pulled a slim, pale-covered book from a shelf nearby and offered it without checking the title. "Try this one."

She looked at him with those fire-ring eyes and accepted it. "You haven't even looked at it."

"I don't need to," Patrick said. "It's yours."

Her name was Chloe. He found that out twelve minutes later at the register, when the bookseller said it while handing back her card. Chloe. Of course. It fit her the way a word fits a thing it was invented for.

Two

The Things He Memorized

He ran into her again at the farmers' market six days later — or rather, she appeared beside the tomato stall and his entire nervous system staged a small revolution. He said hello as if they were old friends. She said hello as if she almost remembered him, and then did.

"The bookshop," she said. "The man with the mystery novel."

"Architecture." He corrected, mortified that he'd corrected her.

She laughed — a quick, surprised sound — and he stored it carefully, the way people store things they're already afraid to lose.

"He was not in love with her yet. But he was already the person he would become because of her."

They walked through the market together for forty minutes, and by the end Patrick had catalogued everything: the way she held her canvas bag with both hands when it got heavy. The way she paused at each stall like it deserved her full consideration. The way she laughed first with her eyes — the corners crinkling before her mouth had made up its mind. The small scar near her left eyebrow that she touched when she was thinking.

He was not in love with her yet. But he was already the person he would become because of her, and somewhere underneath the noise of the market, he sensed the ground shifting, and he let it.

Three

All the Distances

They became friends the way fires become fires — slowly, and then all at once.

Coffee on Fridays. Texts about strange things they saw on their commutes. A shared playlist she started called For Tuesday People that had nothing sad in it, only songs that felt like late afternoon light through a clean window. He added to it secretly at first, then openly, and each addition was a small declaration neither of them named.

Patrick had been in love before — or something adjacent to it. But this was different. This felt cellular. Like his whole body had been quietly reorganizing itself around her without asking permission. He noticed it in foolish ways: that he walked faster toward any room she was in. That he saved the good stories for her. That her opinion of ordinary things — a film, a meal, a piece of music — mattered more to him than his own.

He told his friend Dara about it one evening, trying to sound casual.

"She's funny," he said. "And kind. Not in a soft way — in a deliberate way. Like she chose to be."

Dara watched him. "You're insane about her."

Patrick opened his mouth to disagree. Closed it. "Yeah," he said quietly. "I think I am."

It was the first time he'd said it out loud, and it felt less like confession and more like relief — like finally setting down something heavy he'd been carrying for so long he'd forgotten it had weight.

Four

The Night It Rained

It rained on the night of Dara's birthday party — the kind of rain that makes the city smell like something new. Chloe arrived late, her coat dark-patched with it, and found Patrick standing by the kitchen window watching the street below. He turned when she came in and something crossed his face that he didn't manage to hide in time.

She stopped. "What?" she asked, touching her hair, thinking she looked a mess.

"Nothing," he said. "You're just—" He stopped himself.

"I'm just what?"

For a long moment, Patrick said nothing. The party moved around them — music, laughter, the clink of glasses — but in the kitchen doorway it was very quiet. He looked at her the way she had looked at that book in the shop: like something he was trying to hold without touching.

"You make everything better," he said finally. "Every room you walk into. Every day I know I might see you." He exhaled. "I've been trying to find a smaller way to say it but there isn't one."

Chloe was very still.

"I'm completely, ridiculously gone on you," Patrick said. "I have been for a while. I know that might be — I know it's a lot. I'm not trying to make it your problem. I just couldn't—" He stopped. "I couldn't not say it anymore."

The rain against the window. Somewhere behind them, someone laughed at a joke they'd missed entirely.

Chloe crossed the kitchen and kissed him — just briefly, just once — and pulled back to look at his face.

"I added seventeen songs to that playlist," she said softly, "and every single one was for you."

Five

What Happened After

What happened after was not dramatic. It was better than that.

It was Sunday mornings with the newspaper divided into territories. It was Chloe reading aloud from books she loved, her voice rising at the good parts, Patrick watching her instead of listening. It was the playlist growing into something neither of them had planned — four hundred songs, each one a small memory, a date, an ordinary afternoon made permanent.

He stayed insane about her. That never changed. He was insane about the way she negotiated with houseplants, addressing them by name, gently scolding the fern. Insane about her certainty — the way she knew what she thought, and said so, and was also capable of changing her mind when given good reason. Insane about the fact that she had chosen him, which continued to strike him, daily, as one of the great inexplicable gifts of his life.

"She had not rescued him or completed him. She had simply made being himself feel like enough."

And Chloe — Chloe, who had put down the devastating novel because she'd been in a Wednesday mood — found that loving Patrick was the most Wednesday thing she'd ever done. Hopeful. Not too loud. A light through a clean window, steady and warm and never once unkind.

She had not rescued him or completed him. She had simply made being himself feel like enough. And he had done the same for her, in his particular, ardent, slightly-too-intense way.

There are people who move through the world like weather — changing everything they touch, and unaware of it. Chloe was one of those people. And Patrick, who noticed everything, who had been watching from the beginning, was simply grateful to stand in the middle of it, drenched and not minding at all.

"You make everything better. Every room you walk into. Every day I know I might see you."
